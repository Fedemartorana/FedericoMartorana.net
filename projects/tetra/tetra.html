tetra

when Jun. 2025 why In Naples, in San Carlo all’Arena, behind the Albergo dei Poveri, seven towers inspired by folklore outline a single cultural park composed of three areas, different in nature but united by the same purpose: to enliven and reactivate memory. This is Tetra®. The nearby Botanical Garden, the city, and the Albergo are brought together in a project that lightens the burden of culture and the machinery that sustains it, dissolving the traditional unity of space and function and scattering them throughout a deliberately open and free environment. The area beneath the pre-existing pavilion—designed to engage with the city through spaces for recreational and workshop activities, alongside exhibition areas animated by panoramic walkways—invites visitors to discover the archive, housed within the four central towers and accessible underground. This descent reconnects with the level of the Albergo, linking its rear façade to the Garden, incorporated in the third and final design realm: a green area with scattered trees, completing the triad of gestures—reworking (the pavilion structure), constructing (the archival system), preserving (the botanical park). These gestures belong equally to the field of architectural design and to that of Memory, the very heart of Tetra®. Seven burnished steel figures (Pulcinella, ‘o Guappo, ‘a Vecchia, ‘o Scartellato, Masaniello, Paglietta, Putipù), with their shifting aura, draw a fixed and preordained scheme. Upon this scheme—like on a game board—the visitor enacts their own personal freedom, living unique and educational experiences. The creation of a singular place composed of spaces conceptually coherent yet empirically distinct (since their logic precedes and transcends function) forces the visitor to experience and discover them, placing them in a state of crisis: a condition where a question arises, and the answer must be sought within the space itself. Just as Naples, despite its rigid urban frameworks, always finds ways to compose its slow, chaotic ferment, here too the vulgarization—that is, the re-appropriation into a popular and everyday dimension—of the archive and the space of study restores them to the sphere of leisure. It reminds us that study (from studium) must be a passion, and that research can be engaging and even playful, like the various experiences and attractions within the pavilion, all consistent with the vibrancy of the urban fabric. By extending thematic axes across the site to bring back three key aspects of the city—play, sport, and the market—the project recreates the Neapolitan atmosphere, stitching together fragments of urban fabric that had frayed over time. We developed Tetra because, for us, Architecture is not merely the design of built space: it is a total cultural act. It is a project of meaning, form, and relation. The archive we envisioned was born from this drive, from an idea of architecture that transcends walls to inhabit the languages of communication, technology, and design. We chose not to limit ourselves to the design of a building, but to construct a complex system that embraces its presence in the world we live in. We designed the archive’s website, wrote its code, built its visual identity, and conceived its communication supports—from the logo to the merchandising, from billboards to posters, to signage. Every element belongs to the same design gesture. As Hans Hollein declared, “Everything is architecture.” A manifesto that is no longer just provocation, but method. Thus, the archive becomes a living device: not only a container of memory, but a project of the future. In an era where disciplinary boundaries dissolve, our work has taken on the responsibility of restoring to design its most radical and integral dimension: one that unites space and sign, matter and interface, content and form. Because architecture is in everything. And everything is architecture.
